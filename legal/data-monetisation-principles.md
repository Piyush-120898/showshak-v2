# ShowShak — Data & Monetisation Principles

**Version:** [VERSION] · **Status:** Internal principles (with an optional public summary). Beta — India only.

> This note states the principles that govern how ShowShak treats data and how it
> intends to monetise. It exists so that our **public legal documents**, our
> **product**, and our **investor narrative** never contradict each other. It is a
> commitment, not a marketing claim. Anything here that becomes a public-facing
> promise must be reflected in the Privacy Policy and reviewed by counsel.

---

## 1. The one line

**The user is not the thing we sell. We monetise aggregated, de-identified taste and
intent — never an individual's personal data, and never one platform's secrets sold
to its rival.**

## 2. What we will never do

- **Never sell personal data** that identifies an individual.
- **Never** expose the "scoreboard": no public fires-received totals, no public
  Watch-It taps, no individual's "fires given" (enforced in the database by
  row-level security).
- **Never** sell one streaming platform's private performance to a competitor as a
  "spy report".
- **Never** re-identify individuals from aggregated/de-identified data.
- **Never** repurpose personal data for a materially new purpose (e.g. ad targeting)
  without an updated Privacy Policy, a new version, and fresh consent where the law
  requires it.

## 3. What we may do (the lawful monetisation path)

- Build and share **aggregated, de-identified market and audience insights** — for
  example, "crime thrillers are surging in India this quarter," or "this title is
  converting attention into Watch-It taps across platforms."
- Sell each platform **its own mirror**: how it performs in the open cross-platform
  market — a product only a neutral layer can offer, and one that does not betray any
  competitor.
- Later, and only on opt-in, clearly-labelled, rate-limited terms: **promoted clips**
  and **performance user-acquisition** partnerships (see the Privacy Policy
  "Advertising and monetisation status" section and the Curator Terms "Promoted
  clips" section).

## 4. The four rules that keep us safe (Nielsen/Parrot-style)

1. **Aggregate trends, never raw competitive intelligence.** Market signals everyone
   benefits from — not any one party's confidential numbers.
2. **Neutrality is the product.** The moment we are seen to favour one platform, the
   data loses value to everyone and we lose supply. Protect the neutral reputation
   like it is the company.
3. **Sell each platform primarily its own mirror** — highest value, lowest conflict,
   politically clean.
4. **Anonymised / audience-level only** — never a leak of any rival's internal
   dashboard, and never anything that re-identifies a person.

## 5. The trust tax (the discipline that makes it work)

The moment we push content users can't watch without paying, or lean too hard on
monetised/promoted content, we risk the trust the whole product runs on. We guard the
ratio of organic to monetised/promoted content deliberately (a hard in-product cap,
indicatively ~15–20%), and promoted clips are always real curator clips, labelled and
taste-matched. Trust is the inventory; we do not strip-mine it.

## 6. Legal anchors (confirm with counsel)

- **DPDP Act, 2023 + DPDP Rules, 2025:** consent + purpose limitation; aggregated/
  de-identified data falls outside "personal data" once it cannot re-identify a
  person; new purposes require fresh notice/consent.
- **Disclosure:** promoted/sponsored content must be labelled (ASCI guidelines; IT
  Rules). 
- **How the majors do it (reference, not a template to copy):** Google and Meta both
  state they do not sell personal data and monetise via internal ad-matching plus
  aggregated/de-identified measurement and insights reporting to partners. Our model
  is the neutral, cross-platform version of the same lawful pattern.
